## The good years

There once was a time when I did not care what grade I got on a test. When I studied jazz Piano, getting a C on some exam testing my ability to remember some facts about some random composer I never heard of definitely did not feel like a "big deal".

## The valley of despair

Then I got into computer science and mathematics and all of a sudden grades started to matter a lot more to me. I felt like the only way to prove my worth was by getting good grades and I felt like an absolute badass. Then a funny thing happened: getting good grades did not change anything. Having a 9.7 CGPA did not help me find a job and I was certainly not getting any financial help because of my performance. Then the question shifts from "How do I get good grades ?" to "Is getting good grades the best use of my time ?".

Most of today's exams are pretty straightforward and pretty forgiving. Standards are slipping and grades are climbing, which results in a devaluation of exceptional performance. When everyone is special, no one is. So clearly the first question is not a hard one to answer. The second one is much more finicky though.

## Grades do matter

It seems like the value of getting good grades has been deteriorating while the value of personal projects is climbing. But the value trade-off depends on what your goals are. If research is your primary focus, then grades still matter since you're going to be applying to academic positions and professors will make a judgment based off of your grades. Your grades can also act as a barrier for teaching assistantships and research intern positions. So

## Actually they don't

But for quite literally anything else, your projects and experiences will matter a lot more than your ability to regurgitate information on a midterm. Which is conflicting for someone like me who's interested in both depth and real world experience.

## University

Although the value of University degrees has been going down over time, I still think attending University is worth it. You get a lot more exposure out right and get an instant credibility boost when applying for jobs. But the less hard it is to get an A+, the less valuable having a high CGPA is for employers.

This was more of a rant than a well thought-out essay. I do not hold the solution for these issues and am still trying to come to terms with the conundrum.
